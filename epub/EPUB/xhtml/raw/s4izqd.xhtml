Neostigmine in palliative care patients should only be used under the direction of a palliative care specialist. Speak to one before starting neostigmine.

Indications:

  • Acute colonic pseudo-obstruction in which supportive therapy has failed
  • Ileus
  • Severe constipation refractory to standard therapy

Contraindications:

  • Mechanical obstruction of intestine or urinary tract
  • Colon perforation
  • Peritonitis 
  • Active bronchospasm
  • Known hypersensitivity to neostigmine
  • Cardiac disease (bradycardia, arrhythmia, recent MI within 3 months)

Precautions:

  • Systolic blood pressure < 90mmHg
  • Asthma
  • Epilepsy
  • Parkinsonism 
  • Recent intestinal or bladder surgery

Before starting:

  • Exclude a bowel obstruction (e.g. with a CT scan of the abdomen/pelvis) and other contraindications

Adverse effects:

  • Documented adverse effects are more common with intravenous compared to subcutaneous administration
  • Common adverse effects with subcutaneous administration:
    • Bradycardia
    • Abdominal cramping, nausea, vomiting and diarrhoea with persistent abdominal distension
  • Adverse effects uncommon with subcutaneous administration
    • Hypotension
    • Increased salivation
    • Increased sweating
    • Bronchial secretions
    • Bronchospasm
    • Respiratory depression
    • Fasciculations, muscle weakness

Commencing neostigmine:

  • Inject 0.5mg subcutaneously twice a day

Monitoring requirements:

  • Check heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure and pulse oximetry 4-hourly
  • Discontinue neostigmine if bradycardia < 50 beats per minute occurs
  • Atropine can be used to reverse the muscarinic effects (bradycardia with associated hypotension) 

Further considerations:

  • Co-administration of glycopyrrolate (anticholinergic) may reduce risk of adverse effects including bradycardia and bronchoconstriction
