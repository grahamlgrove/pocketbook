Clonidine in palliative patients should only be used under the direction of a palliative care specialist. Speak to one before initiating clonidine.

Indication:

  • Pain (especially neuropathic pain) refractory to other analgesia
  • Refractory agitation or hyperactive delirium in the imminently dying patient (especially if antipsychotics are best avoided)

Contraindications and precautions:

  • Bradycardia or heart block - contraindicated
  • Hypotension
  • Conditions that may be worsened by bradycardia or hypotension (for example, recent acute coronary syndrome, cerebrovascular disease)
  • Raynaud’s phenomenon (may worsen)
  • Renal impairment (use lower doses)
  • Diabetes (may cause transient rise in blood glucose level)
  • Depression (may be exacerbated)
  • Patients taking mirtazapine (can reduce or prevent effect of clonidine due to α2 antagonism)

Before starting:

  • Exclude contraindications by checking blood pressure and heart rate (if appropriate), and consider an ECG
  • If using for pain, record a pain score prior to commencing loading or test dosing
  • Communicate and gain consent

Commencing clonidine:

  • Give an initial loading or test dose of clonidine 75mcg subcutaneously
  • Assess response over 1-2 hours
  • If effective
    • Commence a continuous subcutaneous infusion of 150mcg over 24 hours
  • If there was no benefit from initial loading or test dose after 2 hours
    • Give a second loading/test dose of clonidine 75mcg subcutaneously
    • Assess response over 1-2 hours
    • If effective, commence a continuous subcutaneous infusion of 300mcg over 24 hours
  • If no benefit, further doses are unlikely to be helpful and clonidine should be stopped
  • If renal impairment (CrCl <30mL/min), consider halving the above CSCI doses

Increasing the dose:

  • If necessary, increase the dose of the infusion by 150mcg over 24 hours every 24-48 hours up to 900mcg over 24hours - monitor blood pressure or heart rate prior to any dose increase
  • The typical effective dose is 300-600mcg over 24 hours

Converting to oral clonidine:

  • There is little evidence to guide the duration of treatment
  • If longer-term use is desired, conversion from subcutaneous to oral clonidine could be considered
  • The oral bioavailability of clonidine is approximately 70-80% - a reasonable dose conversion would be 0.75:1 to 1:1 

Stopping clonidine:

  • Use caution when stopping clonidine abruptly after long-term treatment as there is a risk of a severe withdrawal syndrome (which can be worsened if also taking beta-blockers)
  • Wean over 2-4 days (if subcutaneous infusion) or 7 days (if using oral clonidine)

Adverse effects:

  • Cardiovascular
    • Hypotension
    • Bradycardia
  • Neurological
    • Sedation/fatigue
    • Nightmares
  • Gastrointestinal
    • Dry mouth
    • Constipation
    • Nausea, vomiting
