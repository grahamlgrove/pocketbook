Pruritus has many causes and in a palliative care patient experiencing an itch, there may be no connection between the itch and the palliative illness. Thinking carefully about the cause often helps guide treatment.

Before trying oral medications, try emollients and other measures

General measures
Treat the underlying cause if possible and appropriate
Remove precipitants (e.g. medications)
Stop using soap
Try moisturizer / emollient creams, e.g. menthol in aeueous cream (over the counter)


If the itch might be histamine-related -> try a non-sedating anti-histamine, e.g.

  • Cetirizine 10mg daily regularly or PRN +/- famotidine 20mg twice daily or nizatidine 150mg twice daily

If the itch is related to opioid use -> consider one or more of the following options

  • Converting to a different opioid
  • An anti-histamine, e.g. cetirizine 10mg daily
  • An SSRI, e.g. sertraline (non-PBS indication) or mirtazapine (non-PBS indication)
  • Ondansetron 8gm twice daily (non-PBS indication)

If itch is thought due to cholestatic jaundice -> one of the following agents may be tried:

  • Cholestyramine 4g orally three times daily
  • Sertraline (non-PBS indication) 50mg in the morning
  • Specialist options include rifampicin (non-PBS indication) 150mg twice daily and naltrexone (non-PBS indication)

If itch is associated with dialysis or renal failure despite optimising electrolyte balance -> one of the following agents may be tried:

  • Evening primrose oil capsules 100mg twice daily
  • Very cautious use of low dose pregabalin (non-PBS indication) (e.g. 25mg three times a week)
  • UV therapy (requires commitment and availability to attend multiple times per week)
  • Difelikefalin (IV therapy post-dialysis)

For itch in the last few days of life:

  • Haloperidol 1mg via CSCI
  • Lidocaine CSCI (specialist option)
