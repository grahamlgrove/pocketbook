Prescribing medications frequently used in palliative care (especially ones at the end of life) within the hospital setting is usually relatively straight-forward, however, in the community it can be more problematic because certain drugs are not covered by the PBS (i.e. private prescriptions are required). Some helpful tips for prescribing in the community that are accurate for 2024 are listed below. These tips primarily, although not exclusively, relate to medications for end-of-life care in the home setting. Utilise authority prescriptions to ensure adequate quantities of medication are provided.

For injectable opioids:

  • Injectable oxycodone and fentanyl are not available on the PBS
  • Injectable morphine and hydromorphone are available on the PBS

For benzodiazepines:

  • Injectable midazolam is not available on the PBS
  • Clonazepam injection, oral liquid and tablets are available on the PBS

For antisecretory agents:

  • Injectable glycopyrrolate and hyoscine hydrobromide are not available through the PBS
  • Injectable hyoscine butylbromide (Buscopan) is available on the PBS  through a streamlined authority number 

For co-analgesics:

  • Gabapentin is available on the PBS for refractory neuropathic pain as an authority medication
  • Pregabalin is a stream-lined authority drug for neuropathic pain
  • Methadone is available on the PBS  through streamlined authority numbers for tablet, liquid (Aspen methadone brand for pain) and injection formulations
  • Amitriptyline and valproate are available without PBS restrictions
